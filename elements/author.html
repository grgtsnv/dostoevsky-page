Fyodor Mihaylovich Dostoevsky

Fyodor Dostoevsky (1821â€“1881) was a Russian novelist, philosopher, and journalist, best known for his deep psychological insight and exploration of existential themes. Born in Moscow, he began his literary career with Poor Folk (1846), which gained immediate acclaim. In 1849, he was arrested for his involvement in a radical intellectual group and spent years in Siberian exile. His experiences there profoundly shaped his later works. Dostoevsky's major novels include Crime and Punishment (1866), The Idiot (1869), Demons (1872), and The Brothers Karamazov (1880). His writing delves into morality, free will, faith, and the human psyche, influencing thinkers, writers, and psychologists worldwide. He is considered one of the greatest novelists in world literature.
